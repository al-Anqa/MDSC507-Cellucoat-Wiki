Project Description



The cost of food waste in Canada exceeds $100 billion annually, with 42% of all fruits and vegetables going to waste. Landlocked provinces face unique challenges in preventing food waste as they are reliant on efficient shipment time for maintaining fresh produce. Unfortunately, conventional preservation methods such as drying and freezing can cause significant losses in quality, underscoring the need for a sustainable and effective solution for produce preservation.

Figure 1: Overview of the wet lab project workflow using components 1 and 3.

Bridging the gap between food preservation and food waste is necessary to effectively extend the shelf-life of fruit and vegetables.

Bacterial cellulose (BC), a naturally produced, high-strength network of biopolymers, can maintain nutritional qualities of food without causing physical damage. However, current BC production methods are limited and antimicrobial coatings are not yet effective for all foods.

To increase shelf-life of fruits and vegetables, Cellucoat will use recycled fruit waste to develop a BC-based, edible, preservative coating.

Cellucoatâ€™s preservative properties will come from lysozyme production. Lysozyme is frequently used as an antimicrobial enzyme in packaging materials, and is also found in mammalian secretions. It shows effective antimicrobial activity against Gram-positive bacteria.

Description of the Wet Lab Project

If we go ahead with Cellucoat, our project will be split into two components:

  1. Optimizing bacterial cellulose growth in G. xylinus using fruit waste in HS media.
  2. Producing the components required for bacterial cellulose production in E. coli.

For the purpose of this wiki page, we will replace component 2 with:

  1. Producing lysozyme in E. coli and immobilization onto bacterial cellulose for antimicrobial properties.
